How to sell Flow

Three things the prospect must believe, one pitch per persona, and the discovery questions that open the deal.

Conversation anchors: what the prospect must believe
01
Your planning process is reactive, and it's costing more than anyone has quantified.

Planners rebuild the schedule every morning. Every disruption triggers a manual reaction. Safety stock buffers inefficiency instead of serving demand. Revenue gets turned away because nobody can see real capacity. The cost of this isn't a line item. It's distributed across waste, overtime, missed orders, and customer trust. Most manufacturers have never measured it.

02
No existing tool makes the forecast, plan, and schedule aware of each other.

ERP systems execute transactions. APS tools optimise one function at a time. Spreadsheets fill the gaps. None of them resolve against each other continuously. When something changes, a person has to figure out the impact manually. Flow connects them so they re-solve together.

03
This is provable in weeks, not months.

KLN Foods: live in production. $5-7M projected annual savings. TOMRA: quote response time from weeks to minutes. Industrial OEM: 15 iterations to 1. Three weeks to proof of value. Three months to production. Zero rip and replace. The pattern: real data, real results, fast.

By persona: one pitch each

VP / DIRECTOR OF OPERATIONS

Cares about: throughput, uptime, schedule adherence, planner productivity.

"Your planners spend most of their day rebuilding schedules that are already out of date. Flow makes your forecast, plan, and schedule resolve against each other continuously. When something changes, everything adapts. Your planners spend time improving the operation instead of administering it."

Do not say: "AI-powered optimisation." Do not lead with technology. Lead with what their planners do today.

CFO / FINANCE

Cares about: working capital, waste, margin, capex avoidance.

"Your safety stock is sized on averages, not customer volatility. Your changeover sequences are based on tribal knowledge, not optimisation. Flow right-sizes inventory, reduces waste, and finds throughput capacity you didn't know you had. More output without more lines or headcount."

Do not say: "AI" or "application." Talk in their language: working capital, yield, waste, margin.

CIO / IT

Cares about: integration, security, deployment risk, total cost of ownership.

"Flow is an application that sits on top of whatever ERP and MES you already run. Single-tenant Azure, your own environment. API integration with IFS, SAP, Oracle. No rip and replace. Three weeks to proof of value on your data. If it doesn't work, you've lost three weeks, not three years."

Do not say: "replaces your ERP." Do not oversell AI. This persona wants architecture, security, and risk mitigation.

PLANT MANAGER

Cares about: daily output, disruption recovery, floor-level decisions.

"When a line goes down, your planner replans from scratch. Two hours lost on top of the downtime. Flow presents three options with trade-offs visible. The planner picks one. Minutes, not hours. And when the covering planner runs the floor, the factory runs the same way."

Do not say: anything abstract. This person lives on the floor. Use their language: lines, changeovers, shifts, downtime.

Ideal customer profile

What does a good Flow fit look like?

Use these signals to qualify quickly. If the prospect doesn't match at least three green signals, it's probably not a fit.

Industries

CORE INDUSTRIES

Food and beverage : recipe-based, allergen constraints, changeover-heavy, short shelf life

Chemicals and process : batch sequencing, hazmat constraints, yield optimisation

Metals and materials : furnace sequencing, coil/sheet optimisation, scrap reduction

Industrial equipment : complex quoting, engineered products, long lead times

ADJACENT INDUSTRIES

Automotive components : JIT delivery, multi-site scheduling, supplier coordination

Construction products : project-driven demand, seasonal variability, site logistics

Shipbuilding : long-cycle ETO, complex BOMs, yard scheduling

Packaging : high-speed lines, short runs, frequent changeovers

Production models

Make-to-Stock {MTS}

Produce against a forecast, hold in inventory, ship against orders. Driven by demand accuracy, changeover efficiency, and safety stock management.

EXAMPLES

Snack foods, beverages, confectionery, cleaning products, paints, canned goods, frozen foods, pet food, personal care products.

Make-to-Order {MTO}

Produce against confirmed customer orders. Driven by order grouping, material yield, and delivery date accuracy.

EXAMPLES

Aluminium coils, steel plate, specialty chemicals, custom packaging, industrial gases, glass products, cable and wire.

Engineer-to-Order {ETO}

Each order is a unique design. Driven by specification interpretation, BOM generation, and constraint-validated quoting.

EXAMPLES

Industrial machinery, reverse vending machines, process skids, shipbuilding modules, custom HVAC, conveyor systems, power transformers.

Configure-to-Order {CTO}

Standard product with customer-specific configuration. Driven by configuration rules, pricing logic, and production feasibility.

EXAMPLES

Commercial vehicles, elevator systems, modular buildings, switchgear panels, medical devices, industrial pumps.

STRONG FIT SIGNALS

✓ Manufactures physical products with production schedules

✓ Complex changeover rules (allergens, cleaning, material types)

✓ Multi-site production with shared capacity or customers

✓ High volume of pull-in requests or order changes

✓ Quoting takes weeks with multiple iterations

✓ Planning done in spreadsheets or tribal knowledge

✓ 10+ planners across the operation

✓ Configured or engineered products with constraint-heavy quoting

DISQUALIFIERS

✗ Single product, single line (no scheduling complexity)

✗ No ERP or production data to connect to

✗ Already running a well-optimised APS with high adoption

✗ Pure distribution or retail with no manufacturing

✗ Not willing to share production data for proof of value

✗ Fewer than 5 planners (ROI may not justify)

Buying signals and triggers

These are the events and conditions that create urgency. If a prospect has one of these, the timing is right.

PEOPLE TRIGGER

Key planner retiring or leaving. Covering planner already causing problems. Hiring freeze means existing team needs to do more with less.

GROWTH TRIGGER

Acquisition creating multi-site complexity. New product lines added. Customer base expanding faster than production capacity. New site coming online.

PERFORMANCE TRIGGER

Missed customer commitments. OTIF declining. Customer churn linked to delivery reliability. Board or CEO demanding operational improvement.

TECHNOLOGY TRIGGER

APS evaluation already underway. Frustrated with 12-18 month implementation timelines from traditional vendors. ERP upgrade creating a window of opportunity.

COST TRIGGER

Excess inventory tying up working capital. Waste and scrap above acceptable levels. Overtime costs escalating. Safety stock aging and being written off.

MARKET TRIGGER

Supply chain disruptions forcing constant re-solving. Tariff changes affecting sourcing decisions. Customer demand volatility increasing. Competitors responding faster.

Pains

These are the problems your prospect lives with every day. They may not describe them this way. Listen for the symptoms.

PLANNING IS REACTIVE

Schedule rebuilt every morning. Every disruption triggers a manual reaction. Planners spend most of their day firefighting. Nobody has time for root cause analysis or improvement.

WHAT YOU'LL HEAR

"We replan every day." "The plan is out of date before the shift starts." "Our planners work 12-hour days."

DISCONNECTED SYSTEMS

Forecast in one system. Plan in Excel. Schedule manually checked against capacity. Materials checked in a third system. No single view of the truth.

WHAT YOU'LL HEAR

"We use three spreadsheets." "The demand planner and the scheduler never talk." "Our ERP doesn't do scheduling."

TRIBAL KNOWLEDGE

Changeover rules, customer volatility, supplier reliability: all in one person's head. When they're off, the factory runs worse. When they leave, it's gone.

WHAT YOU'LL HEAR

"Only Dave knows why we sequence it that way." "When the covering planner runs it, we get problems." "We're worried about retirement."

INVISIBLE CAPACITY

The schedule looks full. But nobody knows if it's full of the right things. Safety stock oversized. Changeover sequences suboptimal. Revenue turned away that didn't need to be.

WHAT YOU'LL HEAR

"We can't take that order." "I don't know if we have room." "We always say no to rush orders."

SLOW QUOTING

Engineering builds every quote from scratch. Customer changes one parameter, the entire quote is rebuilt. Weeks pass. Competitors respond faster. Salespeople are waiting.

WHAT YOU'LL HEAR

"Quoting takes three weeks." "The customer changed one thing and we started over." "We lose deals because we're too slow."

BROKEN COMMITMENTS

Sales confirms dates without checking capacity. When the date slips, production gets blamed. Customer trust erodes. OTIF declines. Penalties increase.

WHAT YOU'LL HEAR

"Sales promises dates nobody checked." "Our OTIF is dropping." "Customers are starting to complain."

WASTE AND WORKING CAPITAL

Safety stock sized on averages, not customer volatility. Inventory ages and gets written off. Scrap rates too high. Changeover sequences not optimised. Capital tied up in the wrong places.

WHAT YOU'LL HEAR

"We wrote off $X in inventory last year." "Our waste rate is X%." "We hold too much safety stock."

SUPPLIER VOLATILITY

Supplier delays discovered too late. No alternative plans. Tomorrow's schedule rebuilt from scratch when a delivery doesn't arrive. Tariff changes forcing ad-hoc sourcing decisions.

WHAT YOU'LL HEAR

"We find out about delays the morning of." "We don't have a Plan B." "Tariffs are killing our sourcing strategy."

Technical overview

What Flow is. What it connects to. How it deploys.

Flow is an application. It sits on top of whatever systems the customer already runs. It does not replace ERP, MES, or any existing operational software.

What it is

Application, not a platform.

Flow connects forecasting, planning, scheduling, quoting, and procurement into one system that re-evaluates continuously. It adds an intelligence layer above whatever operational systems the customer already runs.

No rip and replace.

Flow reads from existing systems via API. It does not require migration, replatforming, or data warehousing. The customer's ERP remains the system of record. Flow makes it smarter.

How Flow relates to existing systems

Flow adds an intelligence layer above existing planning and execution systems. It does not replace them. For IFS customers, it maps to Demand Planner and MSO. For non-IFS customers, it connects via API to whatever is in place.

IFS CUSTOMERS

Flow maps to Demand Planner (forecasting) and MSO (scheduling and execution). It does not require either to be deployed. It can work alongside them or as an alternative.

Brian: "SWM is really struggling with Demand Planner. They were looking at two alternatives: modify Demand Planner or look at Flow."

NON-IFS CUSTOMERS

Flow connects via API to whatever ERP or MES the customer already runs. No IFS dependency. JW Aluminum, for example, has no IFS system at all.

Bob De Caux: external authentication for non-IFS Cloud customers confirmed from Q3 2026.

KEY TECHNICAL POINT

Traditional planning runs forecast, then plan, then schedule, then sequence: in order. Flow runs all of them simultaneously and continuously. When something changes (new order, cancellation, volume change, late materials, snowstorm), the entire chain re-evaluates at once.
