### Card 02 · See it run: Quoting

**Animation context:** A commercial developer is commissioning an office HVAC system. Three customer questions come in. Flow presents options back each time.

**Constraint cascade (10 steps):** Load calc → Zoning → Air handlers → Chillers → Ductwork → Diffusers → Controls → Refrigerant → Filtration → Commissioning.

**Initial quote**
> **Quote ready** — Standing quote · ready to send to customer
> - Configuration: 12-zone VRF · 4 air handlers · 2 chillers
> - Cooling capacity: 400 kW
> - Lead time: 9 weeks to site
> - Price: £420k

**Change 1 — "We've added a 3rd floor to the build. Does the design still work?"**
*Developer expanded the scheme mid-design.* Zones: 12 → 16.
- **Option A** — Extend existing VRF system · £520k · 10 weeks · Same system *(selected)*
- Option B — Add separate rooftop unit for new floor · £485k · 8 weeks · Lower efficiency
- Option C — Upgrade to centralised chiller plant · £610k · 12 weeks · Better long-term

**Change 2 — "Tenants are worried about noise. Can we make the system quieter?"**
*Acoustic consultant raised concerns on the top floor.* Max noise: 78 dB(A) → 68 dB(A).
- **Option A** — Sound attenuators + variable-speed fans · £580k · 10 weeks · Minimal disruption *(selected)*
- Option B — Relocate plant to rooftop enclosure · £640k · 11 weeks · Best acoustic

**Change 3 — "Floor 2 is now a data centre instead of offices. How does that change the design?"**
*Tenant mix changed. Precision cooling needed.* Floor 2 use: Offices → Data centre.
- **Option A** — Dedicated precision cooling + redundancy · £720k · 13 weeks · Industry standard *(selected)*
- Option B — Oversized existing system + backup · £660k · 11 weeks · Cheaper, less resilient
- Option C — Liquid cooling for servers + standard HVAC rest · £810k · 14 weeks · Best efficiency

**Chiller detail (drill-down):** Carrier AquaForce 30XWV · 450 kW · 12% headroom above peak load · COP 5.8 at full load · 72 dB(A) at 1m · Carrier · 6-week lead. *Reason: best efficiency-to-footprint ratio for the zoning layout.*

**Footer copy:**
> **Watch Flow quote and re-solve.**
> Most quoting takes weeks, and every change starts the clock again. Flow reads the input, works through the full constraint system, and produces a quote in minutes. Customer changes a parameter, the whole quote re-solves. Works wherever the spec, the drawing, or the tender drives the quote.

## 03 — What changes

**Eyebrow:** Day one · measurable

**Headline:** Three things that *change on day one.*

**Sub:** Not a roadmap. Not a pilot result. What planners and engineers see the week Flow goes live.

**Tabs:** Production Planning & Scheduling | Quoting

### Tab — Production Planning & Scheduling

**01 · Absorb change** *(Service level)*
**Absorb change as it happens.**
Every day starts off-plan. Overnight runs missed quantity, a supplier slips, a line goes down at 13:00. Flow reconciles what actually happened, re-solves the plan, and puts the options on screen. Planner picks.
*Before:* Hours of rebuilding, every day → *With Flow:* Minutes, options on screen

**02 · Throughput** *(Throughput)*
**Produce more in the factory you already have.**
The schedule isn't as full as it looks. Flow sequences against real constraints — changeovers, clean-downs, allergen rules, shift patterns — and surfaces capacity that was always there. More good units, same lines, same people.
*Before:* Capex conversation to make more → *With Flow:* Hidden capacity, surfaced

**03 · Inventory** *(Working capital)*
**Optimise inventory levels and safety stock.**
Most of what's on your shelves is insurance against a plan that can't react. When the plan reacts in minutes, the insurance shrinks. Flow re-tunes safety stock across finished goods, WIP and raw materials. Same service level, less working capital.
*Before:* Cash sitting on shelves → *With Flow:* Stock sized to today's reality

### Tab — Quoting

**01 · Speed** *(Response time)*
**Quote in hours, not weeks.**
Drawing, spec, or tender in. Flow works the full constraint system — materials, manufacturability, sourcing, lead times, margin — and produces an accurate quote the same day. Respond to more enquiries than the competition. Win on speed.
*Before:* Weeks per quote → *With Flow:* Same day, accurate

**02 · Change** *(Win rate)*
**Every change re-solves the whole quote.**
Customer changes a parameter — longer span, different material, tighter tolerance. Today, the clock restarts. With Flow, the whole quote re-solves and the engineer picks from options on screen. Decide while the customer is still on the call.
*Before:* Restart the quote, restart the clock → *With Flow:* Options on screen, in minutes

**03 · Win rate** *(Gross margin)*
**Quote more. Win more. Protect margin.**
When quoting takes a week, engineers chase the winnable ones and inflate prices on the rest to cover what they're unsure about. When quoting takes hours, every enquiry gets an answer priced on real costs. More shots on goal. Margin held.
*Before:* Triaged enquiries, inflated prices → *With Flow:* Every enquiry answered, on real cost

## 04 — A day in the life

**Headline:** A day in a plant. *A cycle in an engineered quote.*

**Lede:** Two ways the same problem shows up. Same factory, same people, two very different days. Flip between them.

**Tabs:** Day in a plant | Engineered quote cycle

### Tab — Day in a plant

| Time  | Without Flow | With Flow |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| **07:00** | **Lead planner arrives.** Yesterday's plan got disrupted half a dozen times. None of it is optimal for today. Planner opens Excel, rebuilds today's schedule, knows they'll do this again tomorrow. *(+1 hr rebuilding)* | **Today's plan already reflects last night.** Flow compared what came off the line against what was planned. Three lines were off. Today's plan is already corrected. Planner reviews, approves. *(Done in minutes)* |
| **08:15 / 07:20** | **Demand shifts.** Two orders cancelled. Another came in larger than expected. Planner scrambles across three systems, then spends the next half hour updating shop orders and republishing the schedule. *(+45 min scramble)* | **Cancellations absorbed. Larger order flagged.** The cancellations adjust silently. The larger order needs a call: safety stock, push another order, or overtime. Flow shows the options. Planner decides. *(Adjusted automatically)* |
| **11:30** | **New order opportunity.** "Can we take it?" Schedule looks full. Planner says no. *(Order turned away)* | **Real capacity checked.** Schedule isn't as full as it looks. Flow finds the room two ways: safety stock that's oversized for that customer, or a sequencing change that lines up with other orders where the materials are already on site. Order taken. *(Revenue captured)* |
| **15:00** | **Quality issue on Line 3.** Third time this week. Planner can't get to root cause — too busy replanning. *(Root cause untouched)* | **Out on the floor.** Planner walks Line 3 to investigate and correct the issue at source. *(Problem solved)* |
| **18:00 / 16:00** | **Planner leaves late.** Day spent rebuilding. Tomorrow will be the same. *(Long day. 0 hrs improving.)* | **Planner leaves after 8 hours.** Flow flagged Line 3 falling behind at lunchtime. Planner spent the afternoon on root cause and got the day's production back on track. *(+3 hrs on root cause)* |

**Summary**
- *Without Flow:* A chaotic day that ate the planner's shift. Most of it at the desk: Excel, calls, meetings to reconcile numbers. One order turned away.
- *With Flow:* Same chaotic day, absorbed into a handful of decisions. Factory ran close to optimal. Planner spent the afternoon on the floor: a recurring quality issue, a slowdown on Line 3, the yield gap on the night shift.

**Stats:** 4 disruptions absorbed · 1 order taken · 1 plan all day · 0 carry-over

*Not every day is this eventful. Flow makes the extraordinary ordinary.*
